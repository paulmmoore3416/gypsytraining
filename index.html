Blue Nose Pitbull - Breed Information

Physical Characteristics

  • Weight: Female 30-50 lbs (ideal for service work)
  • Height: 17-20 inches at shoulder
  • Coat: Short, smooth, blue-grey coloring
  • Nose: Distinctive blue-grey nose
  • Build: Muscular, athletic, agile
  • Life Span: 12-16 years

Temperament & Traits

  • Intelligence: Highly intelligent and trainable
  • Loyalty: Extremely loyal to their handler
  • Energy: High energy, requires daily exercise
  • Courage: Natural protector, fearless
  • Affection: Very people-oriented and loving
  • Work Drive: Strong desire to please and work

Health Considerations

  • Hip Dysplasia: Regular screening recommended
  • Skin Allergies: Monitor for environmental/food allergies
  • Thyroid: Annual thyroid testing
  • Heart: Cardiac screening (congenital issues rare)
  • Eyes: Progressive Retinal Atrophy screening
  • Joints: Maintain healthy weight for joint health

Service Animal Suitability

  • PTSD Support: Exceptional emotional awareness
  • Alert Response: Natural alertness to handler's state
  • Protective Instinct: Balanced protection without aggression
  • Trainability: Excel in complex task training
  • Physical Capability: Strong enough for mobility support
  • Public Access: Calm demeanor when properly trained

Blue Nose Genetics

  • Recessive Gene: Dilution of black pigment
  • Breeding: Both parents must carry gene
  • Rarity: Less common than other colorations
  • Health: No genetic disadvantage from color
  • Standards: Recognized by major kennel clubs
  • Misconceptions: Color doesn't affect temperament

Exercise & Mental Stimulation

  • Daily Exercise: 60-90 minutes minimum
  • Mental Work: Training sessions, puzzle toys
  • Socialization: Ongoing exposure to environments
  • Play: Interactive games build bond
  • Working Tasks: Service work provides purpose
  • Rest: Adequate downtime prevents burnout

Important Medical Milestones for 18-24 Month Old Females

18 Months

Physical maturity reached. Ideal time to begin intensive service training. Spay consideration if not breeding. Full adult vaccinations current.

20 Months (Current)

Prime training age. Mental maturity developing. Focus settling in. Perfect window for advanced task training and public access work.

24 Months

Full mental maturity. Service certification ready. Consistent performance expected. Maintenance training begins.

ADA Service Animal Definition & Rights

ADA Definition

Under the ADA, a service animal is defined as a dog that has been individually trained to do work or perform tasks for an individual with a disability.

  • The task(s) must be directly related to the person's disability
  • Dogs whose sole function is to provide comfort or emotional support do NOT qualify
  • Service animals in training have varying protections by state

Public Access Rights

Service animals are allowed in all areas where the public is normally allowed to go, including:

  • Restaurants, hotels, retail stores
  • Hospitals, medical facilities
  • Government buildings
  • Public transportation
  • Schools and universities

Only Two Questions Can Be Asked

  1. Is the dog a service animal required because of a disability?
  2. What work or task has the dog been trained to perform?

Cannot be asked: About your disability, for medical documentation, for certification/ID, or for demonstrations

Service Animal Can Be Excluded If:

  • Dog is out of control and handler does not take effective action
  • Dog is not housebroken
  • Dog poses a direct threat to health/safety of others

Barking alone is not a reason for exclusion unless excessive and disruptive.

Certification & Registration Options

Important: Certification is NOT legally required under ADA. However, some handlers choose to certify for peace of mind or additional legitimacy.

Assistance Dogs International (ADI)

Gold standard for service dog training programs. ADI accreditation shows highest level of training.

Website: assistancedogsinternational.org

International Association of Assistance Dog Partners (IAADP)

Supports owner-trainers. Offers public access test and handler certification.

Website: iaadp.org

Service Dog Certifications

Online testing and certification for owner-trained service dogs. Tests public access skills.

Website: servicedogcertifications.org

United States Dog Registry

Voluntary registry. Provides ID cards and documentation (not legally required but can help in public).

Website: usdogregistry.org

Training Commands Reference

Essential commands for training and daily work

Basic Obedience

SIT

Verbal: "Sit"

Hand Signal: Flat hand, palm up, raised from waist to chest

Purpose: Foundation position, impulse control, default behavior

Usage: Before doors, meals, greetings, when stopping on walks

DOWN / LAY DOWN

Verbal: "Down"

Hand Signal: Flat hand pointing to ground, sweeping motion downward

Purpose: Settle, extended stay, public access rest position

Usage: Restaurants, waiting rooms, long stays, calming

STAY

Verbal: "Stay"

Hand Signal: Open palm facing dog, held steady

Purpose: Maintain position until released

Usage: Safety, distance work, impulse control

COME / HERE

Verbal: "Come" or "Here"

Hand Signal: Arm extended, sweeping toward body

Purpose: Emergency recall, check-in, return to handler

Usage: Off-leash control, emergency situations, engagement

HEEL

Verbal: "Heel"

Hand Signal: Pat left thigh

Purpose: Walk at left side, shoulder aligned with handler's leg

Usage: Public access, crowd navigation, close control

PLACE / MAT

Verbal: "Place" or "Mat"

Hand Signal: Point to designated spot

Purpose: Go to and stay on designated area

Usage: Boundary training, settle in public, home management

LEAVE IT

Verbal: "Leave it"

Hand Signal: Closed fist shown to dog

Purpose: Ignore item, food, distraction

Usage: Food on ground, trash, other animals, dropped items

WAIT

Verbal: "Wait"

Hand Signal: Open palm (similar to stay but temporary)

Purpose: Pause before proceeding, short duration hold

Usage: Doorways, thresholds, before release to activity

OFF

Verbal: "Off"

Hand Signal: Push away motion

Purpose: Get off furniture, person, or object

Usage: Furniture boundaries, prevent jumping, space management

RELEASE / FREE / OKAY

Verbal: "Free," "Okay," or "Release"

Hand Signal: Sweeping release gesture

Purpose: End of command, permission to break position

Usage: After any stay or position hold

Service & Task Work

WATCH ME / FOCUS

Verbal: "Watch me" or "Focus"

Hand Signal: Two fingers to eyes

Purpose: Eye contact, grounding, interrupt anxiety spiral

Usage: Anxiety moments, dissociation, public stress

PRESSURE / DPT

Verbal: "Pressure"

Hand Signal: Pat chest or lap

Purpose: Apply deep pressure therapy for calming

Usage: Panic attacks, anxiety episodes, nightmares

COVER / WATCH MY SIX

Verbal: "Cover" or "Watch my six"

Hand Signal: Point behind you

Purpose: Position behind handler, watch rear

Usage: Public spaces, hypervigilance support, lines

BLOCK / MAKE SPACE

Verbal: "Block"

Hand Signal: Sweeping barrier motion

Purpose: Create physical space buffer from others

Usage: Crowds, close quarters, boundary enforcement

FIND EXIT

Verbal: "Find exit"

Hand Signal: Sweep toward potential exits

Purpose: Locate and move toward nearest exit

Usage: Panic situations, need for escape route

VISIT / SAY HI

Verbal: "Visit" or "Say hi"

Hand Signal: Point toward person

Purpose: Permission to greet approved person calmly

Usage: Social facilitation, approved interactions

WAKE UP

Verbal: N/A (Gypsy initiates)

Action: Gentle pawing, licking, or nudging

Purpose: Interrupt nightmares, night terrors

Usage: Sleep disturbances, REM detection

NUDGE

Verbal: "Nudge"

Hand Signal: Tap your hand

Purpose: Physical touch for grounding

Usage: Dissociation, spacing out, refocusing needed

Protection & Alert Work

WATCH / GUARD

Verbal: "Watch" or "Guard"

Hand Signal: Point to area to monitor

Purpose: Alert stance, monitor environment

Usage: Home security, vehicle watch, perimeter awareness

SPEAK / BARK

Verbal: "Speak"

Hand Signal: Open/close fist (talking motion)

Purpose: Controlled alert bark on command

Usage: Deterrent, alert, controlled warning

QUIET / ENOUGH

Verbal: "Quiet" or "Enough"

Hand Signal: Finger to lips

Purpose: Cease barking immediately

Usage: End alert, stop alarm bark, de-escalate

OUT / RELEASE (Bite Work)

Verbal: "Out"

Hand Signal: Pull away motion

Purpose: Release bite/grip immediately

Usage: Control during protection work, safety command

FRIEND

Verbal: "Friend"

Hand Signal: Welcoming gesture toward person

Purpose: Stand down, person is approved/safe

Usage: Introduce safe people, de-escalate alert

General & Management

LOAD UP

Verbal: "Load up"

Hand Signal: Point into vehicle

Purpose: Get into vehicle

Usage: Car, truck, or other transport

KENNEL / CRATE

Verbal: "Kennel"

Hand Signal: Point to crate

Purpose: Enter crate/kennel willingly

Usage: Rest time, travel, management

GET BUSY / GO POTTY

Verbal: "Get busy" or "Go potty"

Hand Signal: Point to potty area

Purpose: Eliminate on command

Usage: Before entering buildings, on schedule

TOUCH

Verbal: "Touch"

Hand Signal: Present hand/target

Purpose: Nose touch to hand or target

Usage: Targeting, refocusing, nose work foundation

GENTLE / EASY

Verbal: "Gentle" or "Easy"

Hand Signal: Slow calming hand motion

Purpose: Take treat/item softly, slow down

Usage: Food manners, arousal down-regulation

WORK / VEST ON

Verbal: "Work" (as vest goes on)

Action: Putting on service vest

Purpose: Enter working mode, public access behavior

Usage: Before entering public spaces

OFF DUTY / FREE TIME

Verbal: "Off duty" or "Free time"

Action: Removing service vest

Purpose: End of work mode, play and relax

Usage: Home, designated break times

Training Tips for Command Success

  • Consistency is Key: Use the same word and hand signal every time. Everyone who works with Gypsy should use identical commands.
  • One Command: Say the command once. Repeating teaches her to wait for multiple cues.
  • Tone Matters: Use a calm, confident tone. Yelling = stress. Clear = understanding.
  • Marker Word: Use "Yes!" or a clicker to mark the exact moment she does it right, then reward.
  • Reward Timing: Reward within 1-2 seconds of correct behavior for clear association.
  • Proof the Behavior: Practice in many locations, with distractions, at different times of day.
  • High-Value Rewards: Use best treats for difficult tasks or high-distraction environments.
  • Release Command: Every position command needs a release. She holds position until released.
  • Hand Signals: Dogs read body language better than words. Pair words with signals always.
  • Short Sessions: 10-15 minutes of focused training is better than 60 minutes of half-attention work.

Optimal Nutrition Plan

Fuel for a working service animal

Current Training Phase (18-24 Months)

Caloric Needs

Active Female Pitbull (40 lbs): Approximately 1,200-1,400 calories/day

During Intensive Training: May increase to 1,500-1,600 calories/day

Monitor body condition - should see waist from above, feel ribs with light pressure

Macronutrient Ratios

  • Protein: 30-35% (supports muscle development and recovery)
  • Fat: 15-20% (sustained energy, joint health, coat quality)
  • Carbohydrates: 30-40% (working energy, digestive health)

Feeding Schedule During Training

  • Morning (6-7 AM): 40% of daily food - 2 hours before morning training
  • Evening (5-6 PM): 60% of daily food - after training sessions complete
  • Treats: Account for 10% of daily calories (use training treats strategically)

Recommended Food Types

  • High-Quality Kibble: Look for named meat as first ingredient (chicken, beef, fish)
  • Performance/Active Dog Formulas: Higher calorie density, optimal protein
  • Grain-Inclusive Options: Unless specific allergy, grains provide good energy
  • Quality Brands: Purina Pro Plan Sport, Royal Canin Performance, Diamond Naturals Active, Taste of the Wild High Prairie

Training Treats (High Value)

  • Small pieces of cooked chicken breast
  • String cheese (torn into tiny pieces)
  • Freeze-dried liver or meat
  • Hot dogs (cut small, use sparingly)
  • Commercial soft training treats (Zuke's, Wellness, Red Barn)

Supplements to Consider

  • Joint Support: Glucosamine/Chondroitin (preventative for active work)
  • Omega-3: Fish oil for coat, joints, inflammation reduction
  • Probiotics: Support gut health, especially if stress affects digestion
  • Multivitamin: If home-cooking or concerns about commercial food

Post-Training & Maintenance Phase

Caloric Adjustment

Maintenance Work: 1,200-1,300 calories/day

After intensive training ends, reduce slightly to prevent weight gain while maintaining muscle

Ongoing Feeding Schedule

  • Morning: 50% of daily food
  • Evening: 50% of daily food
  • Maintain 2x daily feeding for digestive health and stable energy

Performance Maintenance

  • Continue high-quality protein for muscle maintenance
  • Keep healthy fats for joint support during daily service work
  • Ensure adequate hydration - fresh water always available
  • Monitor weight monthly - adjust portions as needed

Special Considerations for Service Work

  • Pre-Work: Light meal or nothing 1-2 hours before public access work
  • During Work: Water only, no food (prevents bathroom issues)
  • Post-Work: Full meal after work is complete
  • Long Days: Small high-protein snack mid-day if working 6+ hours

Foods to Avoid

Toxic to Dogs

  • Chocolate, coffee, caffeine
  • Grapes, raisins, currants
  • Onions, garlic, chives
  • Xylitol (artificial sweetener)
  • Alcohol
  • Macadamia nuts
  • Avocado
  • Cooked bones (splinter risk)

Problematic for Training/Work

  • High-fat treats (can cause pancreatitis)
  • Excessive grain-free diets (DCM concern)
  • Too many carbs (energy spikes/crashes)
  • Low-quality fillers (poor nutrition, more waste)
  • Constant free-feeding (reduces food motivation)

Hydration Protocol

Daily Water Needs

General Rule: 1 ounce per pound of body weight (40 oz/day for 40 lb dog)

Active/Training Days: Increase by 50% (60 oz/day)

Hydration Guidelines

  • Fresh water available 24/7 at home
  • Collapsible bowl and water for all outings
  • Offer water every 30-45 minutes during training
  • Monitor urine color - pale yellow is ideal
  • Prevent water gulping after exercise (bloat risk) - small amounts frequently

Signs of Dehydration

  • Dry, tacky gums
  • Skin tenting (skin doesn't snap back quickly)
  • Sunken eyes
  • Lethargy
  • Dark urine
